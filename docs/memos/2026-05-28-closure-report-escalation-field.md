# Closure-report escalation-field convention (fifth atomic amendment draft)

**Status:** draft, recommends-only (memo proposes; no instruction-file changes, no skill creation, no atomic amendment applied)
**Date:** 2026-05-28
**Author:** `safeeval-tracks-architect` (Cowork), via `safeeval-agents:design-memo-author` (mode A) with `engineering:architecture` framing
**Companion to:** `docs/memos/2026-05-24-parallel-cowork-tracks.md` (canonical framework), `docs/memos/2026-05-27-proactive-discovery-generalized.md` (observational lenses), `docs/memos/2026-05-27-design-track-disclosure-debt-prevention.md` (whose §10 adversarial review is the worked example of a track flagging its own recommendation as the failure mode), `handoff/reference/07-dispatch-integration.md` (Dispatch as routing layer above the workflow), `agent/memory/safeeval_dispatch_orchestration_procedure.md` (Dispatch-side low-escalation-bar + auto-chaining procedure adopted 2026-05-28 -- this memo's track-side complement).
**Scope:** codify a closure-report convention that lets the Dispatch orchestrator chain (auto-accept and proceed to phase N+1) or pause (route to Steven) without having to read the full closure transcript of every track. Each track's open questions ship with a structural `escalation:` field whose value is `default-accept` or `route-to-steven`. Three framework-level always-escalate triggers floor the field. The convention is portable to any project using the parallel-tracks framework, not SafeEval-specific.

## 1. Proposal

On 2026-05-28 Steven adopted a low-escalation-bar + auto-chaining operating mode for the Dispatch orchestrator: when a track ships a closure report whose open questions are routine, Dispatch should accept the track's tentative recommendations and chain immediately to phase N+1 without firing a permission round-trip back to Steven; when a track ships a closure report whose open questions are architecturally or materially consequential, Dispatch should pause and route to Steven before next phase dispatches. The orchestrator-side half of that procedure is persisted at `agent/memory/safeeval_dispatch_orchestration_procedure.md`. The track-side complement is missing: today closure reports vary in shape, the "Open questions" section is unstructured prose, and the orchestrator has to read every track's full transcript to decide whether to chain or pause. That is the bottleneck the orchestrator-side procedure cannot remove on its own. As long as the chain-or-pause decision lives in transcript prose, Dispatch is forced into either pessimistic-pause (every closure rolls back to Steven, defeating the auto-chaining design) or optimistic-chain (every closure auto-accepts, with no floor on what gets escalated).

This memo proposes a closure-report convention where every open question carries an inline `escalation:` field set by the track itself per question. Two values: `default-accept` (the track is confident in its tentative recommendation; orchestrator may accept and proceed) and `route-to-steven` (the track is flagging architectural or material consequence; orchestrator must escalate to Steven before next phase dispatches). Three framework-level always-escalate triggers floor the field regardless of track confidence: (a) the track explicitly flagged adversarial-review concern about its own recommendation in the same memo's §10-equivalent; (b) the decision materially changes a public artifact (`README.md`, public memos in `docs/memos/`, the live app, application materials); (c) the decision crosses a project boundary (affects Project Arbiter, modifies the parallel-tracks framework outside an atomic amendment, modifies pre-approved permissions). Tracks that ship reports without the `escalation:` field on every open question fail the closure ritual -- orchestrator returns the dispatch to the track for re-format rather than guessing the field's value.

Surfaces touched if the framework refinements ship: `docs/memos/2026-05-24-parallel-cowork-tracks.md` §6 (dispatch-closure ritual gains the escalation-field requirement), new §6.X subsection codifying the three always-escalate triggers, §4.9 (per-track lens column references the escalation field where the lens produces material decisions -- especially design's hiring-reader frame and qa's audit reports), §13 amendment-log entry as the fifth atomic amendment. No code surfaces. No new skill. No instruction-file mirror changes Cowork-side beyond what the §6 amendment cascades to (those are post-ship paste-to-UI steps for Steven, not architect-applied changes).

## 2. Alternatives considered

- **A. Binary `escalation:` field per open question (`default-accept` / `route-to-steven`), three always-escalate triggers as framework floor, per-question granularity.** The proposal above. Each open question carries inline metadata; the field is set by the track; the triggers floor the field; the orchestrator reads the structural field and acts without parsing prose.

- **B. Ternary field with a third `ask-orchestrator` value.** Same shape as A, but with a third value meaning "the track is not sure whether this rises to Steven's level; the orchestrator should adjudicate." The argument is that some questions live in a middle zone where the track is genuinely uncertain about escalation severity, and a binary forces a false choice.

- **C. Per-phase escalation rather than per-question.** One escalation value per closure report covering all open questions collectively. The argument is that phase boundaries are the natural unit of decision-flow, that per-question granularity is over-engineered for a small number of questions per closure, and that the closure as a whole is what Dispatch acts on.

- **D. Status quo + orchestrator reads the transcript.** Do nothing convention-level. Trust the orchestrator to read each track's closure report, decide chain-or-pause based on the prose content, and accept the cost of either reading every full transcript or guessing wrong sometimes.

- **E. Track-confidence-only field, no framework floor.** Same as A but without the three always-escalate triggers. The track's `default-accept` is sufficient on its own; the orchestrator trusts the track's call without a framework-level override.

## 3. Evaluation

**A. Binary `escalation:` field, per-question, with three always-escalate triggers as floor.** Lockstep cost is modest: §6 closure ritual gains one required field on every open question; §6.X new subsection codifies the three triggers; §4.9 lens column annotated where the lens produces decisions material enough to warrant the field. Cognitive load on each track is one extra mental step per open question (mark it `default-accept` or `route-to-steven`), with the framework floor doing the work of catching cases where the track's own confidence is wrong. Buys: the structural field is parseable without prose-reading, so the orchestrator can chain without round-tripping to Steven, and the framework floor catches the failure mode where a track is confident in something Steven would still want to see (because confident-but-architecturally-consequential is exactly the shape of the seven-instance disclosure-debt pattern from the §10 adversarial review of `2026-05-27-design-track-disclosure-debt-prevention.md`). Costs: a binary field forces tracks into a yes/no choice on questions where uncertainty about escalation severity is itself a signal. Mitigation: the framework floor handles the most-important "I am uncertain but the artifact is public" case automatically -- a public-artifact-touching decision always routes to Steven regardless of the track's confidence. So uncertainty maps to the floor, and the track's binary call is about routine-vs-material in the non-floor cases only. The per-question granularity catches the mixed case where one closure has three routine open questions and one consequential one -- per-phase would batch them.

**B. Ternary field with `ask-orchestrator` as a third value.** Lockstep cost is similar to A (one field, three values). Cognitive load is slightly higher -- the track must now distinguish "confident default-accept" from "uncertain ask-orchestrator" from "this needs Steven." Buys: the third value lets the track defer escalation severity to the orchestrator, which is sometimes the right call (the orchestrator has cross-track visibility the track lacks). Costs: in practice the `ask-orchestrator` value is a punt -- it pushes the chain-or-pause decision from the track to the orchestrator without giving the orchestrator any more information than the track had. The orchestrator's tools for adjudicating are exactly the framework floor (does it touch a public artifact? does it cross a project boundary? did the track flag adversarial concern?) -- which the binary field's floor already encodes. The third value duplicates the floor mechanism without adding information. There is also a slippery-slope risk: tracks may default to `ask-orchestrator` to avoid the binary judgment, turning the orchestrator into the de-facto first reader of every question's escalation severity and recreating the bottleneck the convention is designed to remove. The binary field forces the track to make the call, with the floor as the safety net -- which is the architecture this memo wants.

**C. Per-phase escalation rather than per-question.** Lockstep cost is lower than A (one field per closure report instead of N fields per open-question count). Cognitive load is lower per closure. Buys: simpler convention; less metadata to maintain; phase boundaries are natural decision-flow units. Costs: a closure report routinely ships three to six open questions of mixed severity. Per-phase forces the track to set the closure-level field to the maximum of its question-level severities (any single `route-to-steven` question collapses the whole closure into a pause) or to the minimum (any single `default-accept` question forces the whole closure to chain, swallowing the consequential ones). Both failure modes are observable today: the orchestrator-side procedure's reason for per-question granularity is exactly that it lets routine questions chain while consequential questions in the same closure escalate independently. The §10 adversarial review of `2026-05-27-design-track-disclosure-debt-prevention.md` had seven open questions of mixed severity; collapsing them to one phase-level field would have either pessimistically paused on every question (defeating the auto-chaining design) or optimistically chained through the §9.4 hiring-reader-test-administration question that Steven explicitly overruled (which was the question where adversarial review flagged the architect's recommendation as the failure mode). Per-question granularity is what made Steven's override visible; per-phase would have buried it.

**D. Status quo + orchestrator reads the transcript.** Lockstep cost is zero. Cognitive load on tracks is zero (no new convention). Costs: the orchestrator has to read every full closure transcript to make the chain-or-pause call, which (a) is exactly the bottleneck the auto-chaining design is supposed to remove and (b) is unstructured-prose-parsing, which is the same failure mode as the pre-§4.9-lens proactive-discovery gap -- it relies on the orchestrator catching what the track did not surface structurally. The §4.9 generalization argued that what a track structurally surfaces matters more than what the next reader can extract from prose; the same argument applies to escalation. D ratifies the bottleneck.

**E. Track-confidence-only field, no framework floor.** Lockstep cost is similar to A but without §6.X. Cognitive load is the same per question. Buys: maximum track autonomy; no framework-level override. Costs: the §10 adversarial review pattern -- a track confidently recommends an approach that its own adversarial review flagged as the failure mode -- becomes invisible to the orchestrator. A track that has flagged its own recommendation as the failure mode in §10 and then marks the corresponding open question `default-accept` is exactly the shape of the seven-instance disclosure-debt pattern: confident-but-architecturally-consequential. Without a framework floor, the orchestrator chains through that question and Steven never sees it. The §9.4 override in the fourth atomic amendment (hiring-reader-test hybrid administration) is the worked example: the architect was confident in pure self-administered; the source memo's §10 adversarial review flagged self-administration as the failure mode; Steven overruled to hybrid. Without the framework-level "adversarial-review concern about own recommendation" trigger, that override would not have surfaced.

The recurring axis: **per-question structural metadata + framework-level always-escalate triggers (A) is the smallest convention that removes the orchestrator's prose-parsing bottleneck without losing the safety net for confident-but-consequential decisions.** B duplicates the floor without information gain. C buries mixed-severity questions. D ratifies the bottleneck. E loses the safety net.

## 4. Decision

**ADOPT: A.** Binary `escalation:` field per open question (`default-accept` / `route-to-steven`); three framework-level always-escalate triggers floor the field; per-question granularity, not per-phase; closure reports that ship without the field on every open question fail the closure ritual.

Adopted clauses (specific edits to `docs/memos/2026-05-24-parallel-cowork-tracks.md`):

**(i)** §6 dispatch-closure ritual gains a new clause: every open question in a closure report must carry an inline `escalation:` field with one of two values, `default-accept` (track is confident; orchestrator may accept and proceed) or `route-to-steven` (track flagging architectural or material consequence; orchestrator must escalate before next phase dispatches). Format: numbered list, each item ending with `(escalation: default-accept, rec: <track's tentative recommendation>)` for accept candidates, or `(escalation: route-to-steven, reason: <one-clause reason>)` for escalations.

**(ii)** New §6.X subsection "Always-escalate triggers" codifies three framework-level overrides that force `route-to-steven` regardless of track confidence:

1. **Adversarial-review self-flag.** The track's own adversarial review (memo §10 in design-memo-author shape, or §10-equivalent in another memo shape) flagged the recommendation under this open question as a failure mode. The track is recommending an approach it has already named as risky; Steven sees that pattern before it lands.

2. **Public-artifact materiality.** The decision materially changes a public artifact: `README.md`, public memos under `docs/memos/`, the live app (any change visible at https://safeeval.vercel.app or its post-rename successor), or application materials (anything Steven would show a hiring reader). A change that a hiring reader sees ships only with Steven's explicit sign-off; the auto-chain bypass does not apply here.

3. **Project-boundary crossing.** The decision affects Project Arbiter (the second project Dispatch routes to), modifies the parallel-tracks framework outside an atomic amendment (the framework's own amendments go through tracks-architect with Steven's adjudication; bypassing that path is an out-of-band change), or modifies pre-approved permissions (the permission surface is Steven's, not the orchestrator's, and not the tracks').

If any of the three triggers fires on an open question, the track marks it `route-to-steven` regardless of confidence and records which trigger fired in the `reason:` field. The orchestrator's chain-pause logic reads the `escalation:` value; the trigger details are auditable in the closure report and in the dispatch log.

**(iii)** §4.9 per-track lens column annotated where the lens produces decisions material enough to warrant the field. Specifically: design's lens row gains a parenthetical pointer noting that the hiring-reader frame's first-30-seconds test is exactly the shape of the public-artifact-materiality trigger -- decisions surfaced by the design lens often touch hiring-reader-visible artifacts and floor to `route-to-steven` automatically. Qa's lens row gains a parenthetical pointer noting that audit reports' P0/P1/P2 severity grades map structurally to escalation: P0 findings against a visible-surface dispatch floor to `route-to-steven` (public-artifact materiality); P1 findings carry `default-accept` with a tentative recommendation unless an adversarial-review concern is named; P2 findings are routine `default-accept`. The other tracks (policy, ops, orchestrator, tracks-architect) carry the field per the general rule with no lens-specific annotation; their lenses are not as systematically tied to public-artifact materiality.

**(iv)** §13 amendment-log entry as the fifth atomic amendment.

Specific reasoning: the orchestrator-side procedure for low-escalation-bar + auto-chaining is incomplete without a track-side structural signal of what is chain-able vs pause-worthy. Without the convention, the orchestrator has to either read every transcript (defeating the auto-chain design) or guess (with no floor for confident-but-consequential decisions). The convention's per-question granularity preserves the mixed-severity case that the §10 adversarial-review pattern produces routinely. The three always-escalate triggers map directly to the three categories of decisions Steven has historically asked to see: (a) the adversarial-review self-flag (the §9.4 override in the fourth atomic amendment is the worked example -- Steven explicitly cited the source memo's §10 flag); (b) public-artifact materiality (anything a hiring reader sees -- the README and live app are the canonical examples; the design track's hiring-reader frame already encodes this criterion); (c) project-boundary crossing (Project Arbiter, framework-framework amendments, permissions are decisions whose blast radius extends beyond the tracks' authority). The triggers are a framework-level floor, not the primary mechanism -- in the routine case, the track's `default-accept` is sufficient and the orchestrator chains. The floor activates only when the track's confidence and the floor's criteria disagree, which is the failure-mode case the convention is designed to catch.

## 5. Deferred work / unblockers

- **Empirical calibration over first 30 days.** The exact boundary between `default-accept` and `route-to-steven` is a calibration question; the convention sets the framework but tracks will land on the line through use. Unblocker: review the orchestrator-side dispatch-log after 30 days. If chains are routinely surprising Steven (auto-accepted decisions Steven would have escalated), the convention is too permissive -- tighten by adding triggers or by making `default-accept` require a §10-equivalent adversarial review on every question regardless of memo shape. If pauses are routinely routine (escalated decisions Steven default-accepts without comment), the convention is too strict -- loosen by narrowing the public-artifact-materiality trigger to a smaller surface set. The 30-day window matches the §5.9 observations-triage calibration window.

- **`reason:` field grammar.** The `reason:` field on a `route-to-steven` question is free-text one-clause today. Unblocker: after 30 days of dispatches, scan the reason field for natural clusters. If a small set of stock phrases emerges (e.g. "adversarial-review-flagged", "README-touching", "Arbiter-affecting"), the field can be tightened to a closed-set enum. Premature tightening risks forcing a `reason:` value before the actual distribution of escalation reasons stabilizes. Keep free-text for now.

- **Per-question vs sub-question granularity.** Today's convention is one `escalation:` per top-level open question. A complex open question may carry sub-questions internally (the §9.4 hiring-reader-test-administration question in the fourth atomic amendment had implicit sub-questions about cadence, scope, and trigger surface). Unblocker: if tracks routinely report that one numbered question's escalation severity differs across its sub-questions, sub-question metadata may need to be a convention addition. For now, the track's call is to split a mixed-severity question into two top-level questions rather than ship it with internally-divergent severities.

- **Orchestrator-side relay grammar.** This memo's §9.1 question asks whether `default-accept` allows the orchestrator to skip the SendUserMessage relay entirely or whether a consolidated status update still fires. The track-side convention is silent on relay; the orchestrator-side procedure at `agent/memory/safeeval_dispatch_orchestration_procedure.md` is the canonical surface for that decision. Unblocker is on the orchestrator-side procedure, not this memo.

- **Compound value beyond SafeEval.** This convention is portable to any project using the parallel-tracks framework, not SafeEval-specific. The mechanism (structural field on open questions, framework-level floor, per-question granularity) does not depend on FAF, on AI Trust & Safety, on the SafeEval result card, or on any project-specific surface. If Project Arbiter or a future third project adopts the parallel-tracks framework, this convention ships with the framework and the always-escalate triggers transfer (with the public-artifact-materiality trigger's surface set updated to the new project's public artifacts, and the project-boundary trigger updated to reflect the new project's neighbors). That is the second-half value of this dispatch beyond the disclosure-debt skill bundle filed at `handoff/board/pending/0055`: the convention's framework-portability makes it durable beyond any single project's lifespan.

## 6. Risks named

- **R1: tracks marking `default-accept` to avoid the cognitive load of justifying `route-to-steven`.** The slippery-slope risk where the binary collapses in practice to "always default-accept" because that is the path of least resistance. Mitigation: the three framework-level always-escalate triggers are not opt-in; they fire automatically when their criteria are met. A track that marks a README-touching decision `default-accept` is violating §6.X, and the orchestrator returns the dispatch for re-format. Auditability: the orchestrator's chain-or-pause decision is logged in the dispatch log; if a track's `default-accept` rate is anomalously high relative to its public-artifact-touching rate, the framework-drift scan (§4.4.6) will surface it.

- **R2: the public-artifact-materiality trigger's surface set drifts.** Today `README.md`, public memos under `docs/memos/`, the live app, and application materials are the named public surfaces. If a new public surface appears (e.g., a public-facing API doc, a hosted demo, a new portfolio piece) and the trigger's surface set is not updated, decisions touching that surface auto-chain without Steven's sign-off. Mitigation: the framework-drift scan (§4.4.6 monthly cadence) reads the per-track observations and the dispatch log; a public surface appearing without a trigger update is exactly the kind of meta-pattern the monthly scan catches. Adversarial check: ask "what public surface exists in the repo that is not in the trigger's named list?" once per month.

- **R3: the convention is a prompt, not a tool.** Like all instruction-file clauses, the `escalation:` field's presence depends on the track session honoring the convention. A session that ships a closure report without the field is a violation, but the violation only surfaces at orchestrator triage. Mitigation: the closure ritual's existing structure (Notes back to `CURRENT_<track>.md` section 5; STATE row idle; observation post) is already a sequence of checked steps; the orchestrator returns dispatches that fail the closure ritual for re-format. Adding the `escalation:` field to that checked sequence keeps it in the existing rhythm rather than asking the track to honor a new ritual. The risk is that an early-adoption session forgets the field; the cost is one round-trip, not a missed escalation -- the orchestrator's return-for-re-format response makes the violation visible immediately.

- **R4: the always-escalate triggers proliferate.** Today there are three. If every dispatch surfaces a new "this should always escalate" candidate, the trigger list bloats and the binary's signal-to-noise degrades. Mitigation: trigger additions go through tracks-architect as atomic amendments with the standard alternatives-and-decision shape; they are not added inline by any single closure report. The amendment process is the rate-limiter on trigger proliferation. The current three are scoped tightly: adversarial-review self-flag, public-artifact materiality, project-boundary crossing. Each names a specific failure-mode pattern with empirical evidence; future trigger candidates should clear the same bar.

## 7. Adversarial review

The reviewer this section is written for is Steven reading the memo with a hiring-reader frame: does the convention solve the stated problem, or does it install a new bottleneck while claiming to remove one? The strongest version of each concern:

- **"The framework floor is doing too much work; the track's `default-accept` is reduced to a no-op."** If most decisions hit at least one trigger, the track's binary call rarely matters and the convention is effectively three triggers + a vestigial field. Response: the trigger list is scoped to high-consequence cases; the routine dispatches that drive auto-chain (Stage 2 calibration tweaks, internal SOP refinements, observation-triage outcomes, non-visible classifier-translator memos) routinely clear all three triggers and rely on the track's `default-accept`. The seven-instance disclosure-debt pattern that motivated the §9.4 override is high-consequence by definition -- it touches a public surface (the live result card). The convention's design is that low-consequence decisions chain freely while high-consequence decisions escalate; if most decisions look high-consequence, that is itself a signal about what the project is doing this week, not a flaw in the convention.

- **"The convention itself is a parallel-tracks-framework non-amendment change to the framework's chain-pause behavior; per trigger 3, this very memo should `route-to-steven` everything in it."** Response: yes, and it does. This is a fifth atomic amendment; Steven adjudicates the §9 open questions; the convention does not apply itself until Steven adjudicates and the amendment is applied via a separate dispatch (matching the recommends-only shape of the prior four atomic amendments). The §9 open questions in this memo are all flagged `route-to-steven` per the convention -- they are framework changes by definition.

- **"The convention adds metadata to every open question on every closure report; the closure ritual is already overloaded."** Response: open questions are already enumerated structurally; adding one inline metadata field per question is one suffix per question, not a new section. The cognitive load is "for each open question I am writing, mark it default-accept or route-to-steven" -- which the track already does implicitly when it decides which questions to write up for Steven and which to handle silently. The convention makes that implicit call explicit and structural; it does not add a new decision the track was not already making.

- **"The convention assumes every track ships closure reports with open questions sections, but qa audit reports use a different shape (P0/P1/P2 findings, not numbered questions)."** Response: §4.9 lens annotation in clause (iii) addresses this: qa's audit-report findings map structurally to the field (P0 → `route-to-steven` via public-artifact materiality; P1 → `default-accept` with rec unless adversarial-review concern; P2 → routine `default-accept`). The convention is shape-portable: any structural list of "things the track wants the orchestrator to see" gets the field, whether the list is "Open questions", "Findings", "Decisions needed", or another shape. The format clause in §4 (numbered list with inline metadata) is the canonical shape; tracks may use the field on differently-named structural lists as long as the inline metadata is parseable.

- **"The §10 adversarial-review-self-flag trigger requires a §10 section to exist, but not every track's memos follow the design-memo-author shape with a §10."** Response: trigger 1 is worded as "the track's own adversarial review (§10 in design-memo-author shape, or §10-equivalent in another memo shape) flagged the recommendation". The §10-equivalent clause covers other shapes: an "Adversarial review" section, a "Risks named" section that flags the recommendation specifically, or any structural surface the memo uses to record its own self-critique. The trigger fires on the substance (a self-flagged failure mode), not on the section number. If a track ships a memo with no self-critique section at all and a recommendation that turns out to be the failure mode, the convention does not catch it -- but that is a memo-shape gap upstream of this convention, not a gap in the convention itself. The design-memo-author skill enforces §10 by default; the policy-author and enforcement-designer skills produce structurally similar self-critique surfaces. The gap is narrow.

- **"The convention is reactive (it catches escalations after a track has written its closure); a proactive version would have the track decide escalation severity at dispatch start, not close."** Response: this is alternative D-equivalent from a different angle, and it loses the empirical-evidence-of-the-work that closure-time decisions are anchored in. A dispatch-start escalation severity is a guess about what the work will surface; a closure-time escalation severity is anchored in what the work actually surfaced. The convention's at-closure timing is deliberate.

- **"The convention is the architect's recommendation; the architect's adversarial review (this section) is self-administered; per trigger 1, every question in §9 should `route-to-steven`."** Response: yes -- the §9 open questions in this memo are all marked `route-to-steven` per the convention itself (as a demo of the convention applied to its own closure report, per the dispatch's instruction). The self-administered adversarial review here is the same failure mode that produced the §9.4 override in the fourth atomic amendment; the convention's design accepts that and makes the route-to-Steven path the default for adversarial-review-self-flagged questions.

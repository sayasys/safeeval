## 1. Background

The original PII access posture memo (`docs/memos/compl/2026-05-28-pii-access-posture.md`, commit `f62c34c`) adjudicated two compliance-posture decisions deferred from the data-track and report-generator scoping memos. Decision 1 adopted AWS KMS (envelope encryption via app layer) as the encryption-at-rest scheme for the `unredacted_payload_kms_ciphertext` BYTEA column on `evaluations`. Decision 2 adopted an explicit `pii_reviewer` role check on every legal-audience report generation, with each access logged to a `pii_access_log` table per data-track scoping section 4.5.

Three days later the product directive shifted. Steven's directive ("After we get a database running I want to ensure we do not store PII") prompted the PII zero-storage scoping memo (`docs/memos/2026-05-28-pii-zero-storage-scoping.md`, commit `7f03fa7`), which recommended Tier A: drop the three KMS columns + the `pii_reviewer` role + the RLS view's `pii_reviewer` branches before Phase 3 implementation landed. Steven adjudicated Tier A; the M5 migration commit (`e416f02`) dropped the column, the role, the view branches, and the `raw_input` parameter from the persistence path. The KMS unredacted-column infrastructure no longer exists in the SafeEval tree.

The scoping memo's section 5 noted that the compl memo would need either an in-place amendment or a successor memo to record the supersession of Decision 1; section 10 Q4 recommended the successor-memo shape as a parallel architect-track dispatch after Tier A adopted. This memo executes that dispatch.

## 2. What is superseded

**Decision 1 (AWS KMS encryption-at-rest for the unredacted PII column) is SUPERSEDED.**

The decision is moot in the strict sense that there is no encrypted column to gate. After commit `e416f02`, the `evaluations` table holds the sanitized envelope (JSONB) and the per-row metadata; the three KMS columns (`unredacted_payload_kms_ciphertext`, `unredacted_payload_encrypted_dek`, `unredacted_payload_kms_key_id`) are gone. The encryption-at-rest scheme is no longer a question the system asks; the data the original Decision 1 was designed to protect is not stored.

Concrete sub-decisions superseded:

- **AWS KMS as encryption-at-rest scheme** -- N/A. There is no encrypted column. The KMS module that was planned for Phase 3 (`src/lib/data/kms.ts`) is deleted from the implementation roadmap; the `KMSNotImplementedError` thrown by the persistence path under the original design is removed in the M5 commit. The original memo's section 3 threat-model analysis (AWS KMS vs. Supabase Vault vs. pgcrypto) remains valid as historical reasoning, but the decision the analysis supported has no live consequence.
- **Vercel-OIDC-vs-env-var-fallback verification** (Q5 of the original memo, implied through the data-track scoping memo) -- N/A. The verification was scoped to confirm whether the Vercel-deployed app could retain a stable IAM identity to AWS KMS across deployments. With no KMS dependency, the question does not arise. The Vercel runtime continues to talk only to Supabase and Anthropic; no AWS identity is provisioned for the data layer.
- **AWS account dependency** -- REMOVED from SafeEval architecture. The data-track infrastructure mental model returns to "Vercel + Supabase." No AWS account, no IAM role provisioning, no KMS key policy, no CloudFormation template, no CloudTrail retention budget, no AWS SDK in the dependency tree. The third infrastructure provider that Decision 1 would have introduced is uncommitted.

The supersession is total for the at-rest encryption surface. The original memo's section 3.5 conditions-for-revisit (hosting changes, commercial-path ruling-out, future Supabase KMS maturity, HSM-backed pgcrypto) are now downstream of the zero-storage commitment rather than the encryption-scheme commitment; a future revisit of the at-rest question would have to first revisit the zero-storage decision itself.

## 3. What is preserved

**Decision 2 (explicit role check on every legal-audience report generation) is IN FORCE.**

The role check governs the report generator's legal audience independently of the underlying data sensitivity. The original memo section 4.3 reasoning that justified the explicit role check had three load-bearing arguments; all three survive the zero-storage shift:

- **Principle of least privilege still applies even when data is sanitized.** The original memo's section 4.1 argument was that the role check is the access-control surface and collapsing it into the audience classification would make the audience classification the security boundary -- a routing decision rather than a security decision. That argument is independent of whether the data being gated is unredacted or sanitized. Even sanitized envelopes encode the evaluation result, the disposition, the topic labels, the L1/L2/L3 vocabulary applied, and the rule cascade outcome -- material the legal audience may legitimately need but which is still gated by audience-appropriateness regardless of the per-row PII status. Least privilege gates the audience, not the data.
- **Routing-vs-security decoupling is preserved.** The audience classification (`legal` vs. `t&s` vs. `reviewer`) remains a routing decision made by the report generator based on the report request's audience field. Conflating routing with security under zero-storage would still mean a routing bug becomes a security incident. The role check still decouples them.
- **Auth-gate pattern (`LegalAccessGateError`) remains the implementation.** The Phase 2 stub at `src/lib/report-generators/index.ts:84` (commit `2632a66`) holds the auth-gate surface where Decision 2 lives. The stub is unchanged by the zero-storage shift; the gate continues to throw `LegalAccessGateError` when a legal-audience report is requested without the requisite role. The implementation surface for Decision 2 is the same after M5 as it was before M5.
- **The classifier-feedback loop scoping memo (commit `92b5703`) explicitly mirrors this pattern for `EditorRoleGateError`.** The feedback-loop memo's section 7.2 names the `EditorRoleGateError` pattern as a structural mirror of `LegalAccessGateError`: closed-set reviewer roles, default-deny, throw-on-unauthorized. Section 6 of that memo cites commit `2632a66`'s Phase 2 stub as the model. Decision 2 of the original PII access posture memo is therefore precedent for a separate auth-gate surface in the feedback loop; preserving Decision 2 preserves the precedent the feedback-loop work relies on.

The audit-trail axis from the original memo (section 4.3 second argument) shifts in scope: under zero-storage there is no unredacted-decryption event to log to `pii_access_log`, but the auth-gate decision itself remains an auditable event. The implementation can choose between (a) repurposing `pii_access_log` as a `legal_access_log` recording auth-gate decisions without the unredacted-decryption framing, or (b) folding the auth-gate event into the report record itself (the report's audience + timestamp + caller role become the audit artifact). Both options preserve the audit-trail purpose Decision 2 was designed to serve; the choice between them is a downstream implementation question, not a Decision 2 reversal. This memo's recommendation is option (a) renaming for clarity, but defers the choice to the report-generator implementation track.

## 4. New threat model under zero-storage

The original memo's section 2 threat model identified five threat actors / decryption paths. Most of them evaporate under zero-storage; one new residual risk emerges. Brief inventory:

- **Compromised app server reads unredacted PII** -- N/A. There is no unredacted PII for the app server to read. An attacker with code-execution on the Vercel runtime can read whatever the app reads, which is now sanitized envelopes only. The compromised-app blast radius collapses from "everything the attacker can decrypt during the compromise window" to "everything the attacker can SELECT during the compromise window, which by construction does not include identities."
- **Compromised Postgres host** -- reduced impact (sanitized envelopes only). An attacker with root on the Supabase database server reads the data files directly; the data files now hold sanitized envelopes and per-row metadata, not encrypted ciphertext. The blast radius shrinks from "ciphertext exfiltration that requires a separate key compromise to decrypt" to "sanitized envelopes exfiltrated in cleartext, plus per-row metadata." The exfiltrated content is by construction free of identities; the residual risk is sanitizer false-negatives that landed in the envelope. Capped by sanitizer correctness, not by encryption.
- **Insider threat at AWS** -- N/A. SafeEval has no AWS account dependency. The axis the original memo identified as KMS's strongest differentiator over Supabase Vault is no longer in scope; there is no AWS-side surface for an AWS insider to attack.
- **Compelled legal disclosure** -- SafeEval can only produce sanitized envelopes; original input is gone after evaluation. A subpoena response, regulatory inquiry, or chain-of-custody request that asks for the verbatim prompt cannot be satisfied from the SafeEval store. The legal audience receives sanitized envelopes; the unredacted original is not retained anywhere SafeEval controls. The original memo's section 2 "compelled legal disclosure" axis was the one Decision 1 was thinnest on; the zero-storage answer is structural rather than encryption-mediated -- SafeEval cannot produce what it does not have.
- **Sanitizer false-negative** (REMAINING residual risk). PII slips through the regex tier (or, after Presidio lands, through the regex + Presidio composite) and lands in the sanitized envelope. The leak is permanent in the sense the zero-storage scoping memo's R1 named: the original is gone, so retroactive re-sanitization cannot recover. Mitigation per the scoping memo section 9 R1: bump the Presidio sidecar forward in the data-track roadmap; add a frozen regression-test corpus to `src/lib/data/__tests__/`. Additional mitigation: write-time audit at source via `pii_redaction_log`. The redaction-log entries already capture `redactions[].offsets`, `redactions[].source`, and `redactions[].confidence` per entity per row; spot-checking the log against a frozen fixture corpus identifies sanitizer drift before it lands in production. The audit surface moves earlier in the lifecycle -- write-time fixture testing rather than read-time spot-check against an unredacted original -- but the auditability is preserved.

The threat model under zero-storage is materially smaller than under the original memo. Three of the five original axes collapse to N/A or reduced impact; the residual axis (sanitizer false-negative) is mitigated through better sanitization tooling rather than through encryption-key separation.

## 5. Regulatory posture changes

- **GDPR Article 5 (purpose limitation): stronger story.** SafeEval does not process personal data beyond the immediate classification call; it does not persist the personal data after the classification completes. The Article 5(1)(b) purpose-limitation principle is satisfied structurally rather than procedurally -- "the data was used for classification and then not retained" is a simpler claim than "the data was used for classification and retained encrypted under role-gated decryption for the lawful purposes enumerated in the data-track scoping memo." The original memo's GDPR Article 32 ("security of processing") framing in section 3.1 becomes less load-bearing because the personal data does not persist in a form Article 32 governs; the security-of-processing question reduces to "is the sanitizer correct" rather than "is the encryption scheme correct."
- **GDPR Article 14 (data subjects): minimal.** Sanitization runs at source (at the persistence write path, before the row lands in `evaluations`); the data subject is not identifiable from the persisted envelope. Article 14's notification obligations (information to be provided where personal data have not been obtained from the data subject) attach to data controllers processing personal data; SafeEval does not process personal data downstream of the sanitization. Downstream notification obligations are minimal because there is no downstream personal-data processing.
- **CFAA, robots.txt: unchanged.** These statutes and norms govern outbound interactions with external systems (the OSINT side of SafeEval, not the data-track side). The PII access posture memo never addressed them; the zero-storage shift does not change that.
- **SOC 2 CC6.1: simpler control narrative.** "We don't store the data" is materially easier to evidence to an auditor than "we encrypt the data with these keys, the keys are rotated on this cadence, the IAM role is provisioned through this process, the CloudTrail retention is configured to this window, and the per-decryption audit log is reviewed at this cadence." The CC6.1 (logical and physical access controls) narrative under zero-storage reduces to "the surface being controlled is access to sanitized envelopes via the `reviewer` role and the legal-audience auth-gate; there is no second surface for unredacted data because no unredacted data exists." A hostile auditor flagging the original control narrative for any of the chain-of-dependencies failure modes (IAM drift, CloudTrail gap, key-rotation lapse) cannot flag the simpler narrative for the same reasons because the surfaces those failure modes attack do not exist.

The posture changes are uniformly in the simpler-and-stronger direction. None of the original memo's regulatory framings are weakened; several are strengthened by being structurally rather than procedurally satisfied.

## 6. What stays in force from the original memo

Inventory of what remains live after the supersession of Decision 1:

- **Decision 2 (explicit role check on every legal-audience report)** -- in force per section 3 above. The auth-gate surface, the closed-set role definition, the throw-on-unauthorized behavior, and the audit-trail purpose all survive the data-shape change.
- **Manual ops-runbook auth gate at MVP scale** (Decision 3 from the original memo's open question 6.3 -- the recommended lightest-tier auth surface for Decision 2 to depend on) -- in force. The original memo's section 5.2 named three auth-surface options (manual ops-runbook approval gate, session-bound role-elevation surface, full RBAC) and recommended the manual ops-runbook gate at MVP. That recommendation does not change under zero-storage; the auth surface continues to be the manual ops-runbook gate at MVP scale, with the future RBAC promotion path preserved.
- **Future RBAC promotion path** -- in force. The original memo's section 5.2 reasoning that the manual ops-runbook gate at MVP scale should preserve the option to promote to a full RBAC system without re-architecting remains. The promotion trigger (legal-audience report volume becomes high enough to justify it; the original memo section 4.4 named the volume threshold qualitatively) is unchanged.
- **Permission matrix concept (role X can access surface Y)** -- in force, just applied differently. The original memo implicitly carried a permission matrix for the encryption-data gates (`pii_reviewer` role accesses unredacted column; `reviewer` role accesses sanitized envelope). Under zero-storage, the same matrix concept applies to the report-generator's legal audience (legal-audience-permitted roles access legal-audience report generation) and to the feedback-loop reviewer edits (`senior_reviewer`, `policy_lead` roles per the feedback-loop scoping memo section 6 permission matrix). The matrix shape -- closed-set roles, default-deny, per-surface authorization -- carries forward; the surfaces it governs shift from encryption gates to report-generation and edit gates.

The five-item inventory captures everything the original memo committed to that survives the supersession. The unredacted-column-specific commitments (KMS scheme, two-key access tier, `pii_reviewer` role, CloudTrail logging configuration, KMS-specific failure-mode handling per the original memo section 5.1) are gone; the access-pattern and auth-surface commitments survive intact.

## 10. Adversarial review (self-applied at draft time per mode C)

### 10.1 Framework friction observations for the eventual eighth atomic amendment

This is the first successor compl memo (vs. in-place amendment) in the `docs/memos/compl/` tree. Two framework-friction observations surface during authoring; surfaced here so the eighth atomic amendment authoring, when Steven dispatches it, has empirical evidence to draw from.

First, the successor-memo-vs-amendment pattern is established by this artifact. The zero-storage scoping memo section 5 named the two options (amendment-in-place or successor memo) and recommended the successor-memo shape via section 10 Q4 default-accept. This memo executes the successor-memo shape. The pattern is now ratified by use; future compl reversals can follow the same shape without re-litigating the choice each time. Recommendation for the eighth amendment: codify "successor memo is the default for compl reversals where the predecessor remains useful as a historical record; in-place amendment is the default for compl revisions that only update non-load-bearing details (typo fixes, reference updates, status header refreshes)." The distinction is the load-bearing-vs-non-load-bearing of the change; the architect picks the shape based on whether a future reader is better served by both memos in audit-readable form or by a single memo with a marked-up history.

Second, the filing convention `<original-date>-<original-topic>-<change-type>-amendment.md` is established by this artifact's filename `2026-05-28-pii-access-posture-zero-storage-amendment.md`. The convention encodes the original memo's date (preserving the chronological audit trail), the original memo's topic (preserving the searchability link), and the change-type (here, `zero-storage`, naming the architectural shift that drove the reversal). The convention is a candidate for codification in the eighth atomic amendment so future successor memos -- whether under `docs/memos/compl/`, `docs/memos/sec/`, or any other posture subdirectory -- inherit the same filing pattern without re-deriving it. Recommendation for the eighth amendment: codify the filing convention as `<original-date>-<original-topic>-<change-type>-amendment.md` for all successor memos under any posture subdirectory.

These two observations are minor and the architect framework absorbed them without resistance during this memo's authoring. The eighth atomic amendment should include them as small hygiene additions in the same way the seventh amendment absorbed the friction points the original PII access posture memo's section 8.1 surfaced.

### 10.2 Strongest case against the successor-memo shape

The strongest counter to the successor-memo shape is the duplication cost: a reader following the audit trail now reads two compl memos for one PII access posture, not one. The predecessor's section 3 KMS analysis and section 4 role-check analysis are both still substantive content; a reader reaching the predecessor first must then read this memo to learn that section 3 is superseded; a reader reaching this memo first must read the predecessor to learn what section 3 said. The in-place amendment shape would avoid the round-trip -- a single memo with a marked-up history is one read, not two.

**Refutation.** Three points:

(a) The predecessor's section 3 analysis is historically valuable independent of the live decision. The threat-model comparison across AWS KMS, Supabase Vault, and pgcrypto is a portfolio artifact in its own right -- it demonstrates the depth the original memo invested in encryption-scheme adjudication, and a hiring reader scanning the repo for compl-reasoning depth benefits from finding the full analysis rather than a marked-up shell. Preserving the analysis in audit-readable form is a feature, not duplication cost.

(b) The two-read cost is real but bounded. A reader reaching either memo finds explicit cross-references in the predecessor field and section 1 background; the chain across both memos is one click each direction. The audit trail's value is in the chain being readable, not in the chain being single-document.

(c) The successor-memo shape generalizes; the in-place amendment shape does not. Future compl reversals will face the same shape question. Establishing the successor-memo shape as the default here means future reversals inherit a pattern; choosing in-place amendment here means each future reversal re-litigates the question. The framework value of consistency outweighs the per-memo duplication cost.

### 10.3 Strongest case against preserving Decision 2

The strongest counter to preserving Decision 2 is that the role check now gates sanitized envelopes rather than unredacted ones, and gating already-safe data is ceremony without throughput. The original memo's section 4.3 reasoning leaned on principle-of-least-privilege at negligible operational cost when the data being gated was unredacted PII; under zero-storage the data is sanitized, the privilege the check enforces is less load-bearing, and the role check shades closer to ornamental.

**Refutation.** Three points:

(a) The audit-trail purpose Decision 2 served does not depend on the data sensitivity. The auditable event is "a legal-audience report was generated by a caller in role X at timestamp Y"; whether the report contains unredacted or sanitized data does not change the audit-trail value of recording who-generated-what-when. A regulator under inquiry can still ask "show me every legal-audience report SafeEval generated in this window"; the answer remains valuable.

(b) The routing-vs-security decoupling argument does not depend on the data sensitivity either. The audience classification is a routing decision; conflating it with security would still mean a routing bug becomes a security incident, even when the data being routed is sanitized. The decoupling preserves the structural safety property regardless of whether the underlying data is high-stakes.

(c) The `EditorRoleGateError` precedent in the feedback-loop scoping memo (commit `92b5703`) is downstream of Decision 2. Reversing Decision 2 here would foreclose the precedent the feedback-loop work explicitly mirrors; the cross-surface consistency the two auth-gate patterns provide would be sacrificed for an arguable simplification on the report-generator side. Preserving Decision 2 preserves the cross-surface pattern.

### 10.4 What mode C can and cannot do here

Per the design-memo-author mode C rule, adversarial review can only downgrade confidence -- ACCEPT -> PARTIAL ADOPT -> DEFER. The adversarial review above does not flip either of this memo's two main moves (supersede Decision 1; preserve Decision 2); both moves stand at ACCEPT. The counter-arguments are named and refuted on grounds specific to the audit-trail value, the cross-surface precedent, and the framework consistency the successor-memo shape provides.

If the recommendations were overconfident, the mode-C move would be to downgrade the Decision 2 preservation from ACCEPT to PARTIAL ADOPT: preserve Decision 2 as a default-on but flag it for a downstream re-review six months out, on the basis that the data-sensitivity reduction may eventually justify collapsing the role check into the audience classification. This memo declines to do that -- the audit-trail and cross-surface arguments are durable rather than time-bound -- but the partial-adopt path is named for completeness in case Steven prefers the staged re-review.
